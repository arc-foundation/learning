MARGARET HAMILTON

ABOUT

Margaret Elaine Hamilton, born on August 17, Paoli, Indiana, 1936 is an American computer scientist, systems engineer, and business owner. She was director of the Software Engineering Division of the MIT Instrumentation Laboratory, which developed on-board flight software for NASA's Apollo program. She later founded two software companiesâ€”Higher Order Software in 1976 and Hamilton Technologies in 1986, both in Cambridge, Massachusetts. Hamilton has published more than 130 papers, proceedings, and reports, about sixty projects, and six major programs. She invented the term "software engineering", stating "I began to use the term 'software engineering' to distinguish it from hardware and other kinds of engineering, yet treat each type of engineering as part of the overall systems engineering process." On November 22, 2016, Hamilton received the Presidential Medal of Freedom from president Barack Obama for her work leading to the development of on-board flight software for NASA's Apollo Moon missions. Hamilton has a sister Kathryn. She met her first husband, James Cox Hamilton, in the mid-1950s while attending college. They were married on June 15, 1958, the summer after she graduated from Earlham. She briefly taught high school mathematics and French at a public school in Boston, Indiana. The couple then moved to Boston, Massachusetts, where they had a daughter, Lauren, born on November 10, 1959. They divorced in 1967 and Margaret married Dan Lickly two years later.

EDUCATION

Margaret graduated from Hancock High School in 1954 from Michigan. She studied mathematics at the University of Michigan in 1955 before transferring to Earlham College, where her mother had been a student. She earned a BA in mathematics with a minor in philosophy in 1958.She cites Florence Long, the head of the math department at Earlham, as helping with her desire to pursue abstract mathematics and become a mathematics professor. Hamilton initially intended to enroll in graduate study in abstract mathematics at Brandeis University, Boston. However, in mid-1959, Hamilton began working for Edward Norton Lorenz, in the meteorology department at MIT.She developed software for predicting weather, programming on the LGP-30 and the PDP-1 computers at Marvin Minsky's Project MAC.Her work contributed to Lorenz's publications on chaos theory.She moved on to another project in the summer of 1961, and hired and trained Ellen Fetter as her replacement.

CAREER

From 1961 to 1963, Hamilton worked on the Semi-Automatic Ground Environment (SAGE) Project at the MIT Lincoln Lab, where she was one of the programmers who wrote software for the prototype AN/FSQ-7 computer (the XD-1), used by the U.S. Air Force to search for possibly unfriendly aircraft. She also wrote software for a satellite tracking project at the Air Force Cambridge Research Laboratories.

Hamilton then joined the MIT Instrumentation Laboratory, which developed the Apollo Guidance Computer for the Apollo lunar exploration program. Hamilton was the first programmer hired for the Apollo project and in 1965 became Director of the Software Engineering Division.She was responsible for the team writing and testing all on board in flight software for the Apollo spacecraft's Command and Lunar Module and for the subsequent Skylab space station.

During Apollo 11, a critical moment saw the Apollo Guidance Computer facing alarms three minutes before the Moon landing. Buzz Aldrin's request caused an overload, triggering unexpected errors. Margaret Hamilton's "priority display" design alerted astronauts, but introduced a synchronization risk. The alarms resulted from an inadvertently left-on radar switch, causing "executive overflows." Hamilton's software design, with error recovery and priority displays, enabled the astronauts to decide to continue the landing. Jack Garman in mission control recognized errors, shouted "Go, go!" and the mission succeeded. Hamilton's work laid the foundation for ultra-reliable software design, crucial for Apollo 11's success. The computer's ability to recognize and recover from errors was pivotal, as highlighted in Hamilton's 1971 letter titled "Computer Got Loaded."

In 1976, Margaret Hamilton co-founded Higher Order Software (HOS) with Saydean Zeldin, extending ideas from their Apollo program experience. They developed USE.IT, based on the HOS methodology, successfully applied in government programs. Hamilton was HOS CEO until 1984, leaving in 1985. In 1986, she founded Hamilton Technologies, focusing on the Universal Systems Language (USL) and the 001 Tool Suite for proactive systems design and software development.

AWARDS & ACHIEVEMENTS

Margaret Hamilton, a pioneering figure in software engineering, received numerous prestigious awards and honors throughout her career. In 1986, she was honored with the Augusta Ada Lovelace Award by the Association for Women in Computing. In 2003, NASA recognized her with the Exceptional Space Act Award for significant scientific contributions, including a historic monetary prize. Over the years, she received accolades such as the Outstanding Alumni Award from Earlham College in 2009 and the Presidential Medal of Freedom from Barack Obama in 2016. In 2017, the Computer History Museum honored her with the Fellow Award, and a LEGO set featuring women of NASA, including Hamilton, was released. Further recognitions include an honorary doctorate from the Polytechnic University of Catalonia in 2018, The Washington Award in 2019, and induction into the National Aviation Hall of Fame in 2022. Hamilton's extensive list of awards underscores her exceptional contributions to the field of computer science.

Margaret Hamilton's notable publications span various aspects of software engineering and systems development. In 1976 and 1979, she co-authored papers with Saydean Zeldin on Higher Order Software methodology and the relationship between design and verification. In 1994, she contributed articles to Electronic Design on development before the fact and the 001 Full Life Cycle Systems Engineering. Collaborating with W. R. Hackler in 2004 and 2007, they discussed principles of a common software architecture and the Universal Systems Language for preventative systems engineering. Further work in 2008 explored the formal semantics of Universal Systems Language. In 2018, Hamilton delved into the insights derived from software errors in an IEEE Software article. These publications reflect her extensive contributions to software development methodologies, systems engineering, and the lessons learned from the Apollo program.
